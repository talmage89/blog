In existentialism, authenticity is a term for your ownership over your identity.  To live authentically is to lead your existence. It is a way of life in which you not only might live sincerely, expressing outwardly what you feel inwardly, but also in which you live in accordance with your "ownmost" possibilities of life. It describes choosing your most beautiful flight in the face of a cold, reluctant world.

I have been drawn to this term

Authenticity is very closely tied with living "true to yourself." This raises endless questions – who is the self that you are being true to? If the self is the set of actions that you perform, would being true to your self simply be a crystallization of the actions that you've already taken in your life? What dictates what direction your actions should move towards? If your values are what drives the trueness that is sought, would aligning these values to fit your actions also be considered "authentic"? This concept is deeply rooted in the question of what the self exists as.

It's an excellent introduction to the landscape in all its complexity. The Wikipedia articles on [authenticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authenticity_%28philosophy%29) and [existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism) are also a fantastic resource for broader familiarization with the subject. It's easy to talk about being "true to yourself," but understanding the foundation underlying these specific concepts and the knowledge they are built upon leads to a much richer conversation. However, this quickly  leaves the realm of thoughtful reading, diving instead into a space of abstract discussion that expects a deep knowledge of the entire historical conversation up to this moment in history. Here's a few snippets from the AI report that I found insightful:

> Kierkegaard’s famous dictum—“the crowd is untruth”—anticipates Heidegger’s \*das Man\* by nearly a century. The crowd absolves the individual of responsibility by diffusing it. Authentic existence requires becoming \*den Enkelte\*, the single individual, who stands alone and cannot hide in the anonymity of numbers.

Authenticity is fundamentally rooted in the personal experience and with being one's own self.

> The self, for Nietzsche, is not discovered (as in Romantic introspection) but \*created\*. “One thing is needful: to ‘give style’ to one’s character” (\*Gay Science\* §290). The self is an artwork, composed of drives organized into a hierarchy, imposed upon by a sovereign will. Authentic existence is \*sculptural\*: the raw material of one’s instincts and accidents is shaped into a form that one can will again, an \*amor fati\*.

Authenticity is living in an intentional, chosen way.

> Death, for Heidegger, is not an event at the end of life but an \*ever-present possibility\* that constitutes what it is to be Dasein.
> 
> Death has four features that matter for authenticity:
> 
> 1. \*\*Ownmost\*\* — no one can die my death for me.
> 2. \*\*Non-relational\*\* — in dying, all my relations to others are dissolved; I am alone with my existence.
> 3. \*\*Certain\*\* — it is not a matter of probability.
> 4. \*\*Indefinite\*\* — though certain, its \*when\* is unknown.
> 
> Inauthentic Dasein flees death by treating it as an event that happens to others, by evasion, by comforting chatter. Authentic Dasein holds this being-toward-death open as a possibility—what Heidegger calls \*\*anticipation\*\* (\*Vorlaufen\*). Anticipating death does not mean thinking about it morbidly; it means allowing it to illuminate my existence as finite, as \*mine\*, as not-replaceable.

Authenticity is living with full understanding of your constraints, especially mortality. It is rooted in the medium of a limited and unchosen existence.

> Sartre’s famous thesis: we are “condemned to be free.” Freedom is not something we have; it is something we \*are\*. We cannot choose not to choose. Even refusal, paralysis, passivity are choices. Our facticity (body, past, class, language) is not a limit on freedom but the \*material\* of freedom—it is what freedom takes up and transcends.

Authenticity acknowledges freedom is non-optional and can be terrifying – to live authentically is to see this freedom for what it is, and is to truly accept it without falling into busy distraction or false transcendence.

> Beauvoir’s crucial move beyond Sartre: to will one’s own freedom authentically is to will the freedom of others. Unlike Sartre’s \*Being and Nothingness\*, where the Other is primarily an objectifying threat, Beauvoir sees others as the very medium through which my projects become meaningful. My freedom needs a world of free others. Oppression—colonialism, patriarchy, poverty—is not just a political evil; it is an ontological obstruction to authentic existence, because it denies others the conditions for exercising freedom.

> Authenticity, for Taylor, has three conditions:
> 
> 1. \*\*Self-discovery/self-creation\*\*: each individual has an original way of being human that they must discover and enact.
> 2. \*\*Dialogical constitution\*\*: selves are formed in dialogue with significant others; we learn what we are by speaking, loving, struggling with others. Authenticity is not monological.
> 3. \*\*Horizons of significance\*\*: the self cannot create value \*ex nihilo\*. What I can meaningfully be is constrained by the horizons of significance available in my culture; the question “what is worth doing?” cannot be dissolved into “what do I want?”

Authenticity is relational – you cannot live authentically without an environment to be authentic to, or other selves to learn authenticity with.

With this ideological foundation, I hope you can visualize what energy "authenticity" is in your own life and in the lives you observe. I see a frame of mind that looks like knowing, like awareness. I feel a signal of pureness and cohesion, with lives following this being focused and driven. My visualization sees someone embodying this as completely camouflaged in the fabric of the present. They can exist both in it and in the space watching it.

Of course, we must address the negatives of the ideology. The concept has been criticized as an attractive mask for narcissism. Because authenticity focuses so heavily on the individual, philosophy may target this selfish endeavor as not worthwhile, particularly when compared against more socially-oriented ideologies such as utilitarianism or effective altruism. These frameworks define a well-lived life by how completely they helped others, compared to the authentic directive of inhabiting oneself fully. It is very important to take this alternative framing seriously. 

I still believe time spent improving myself is valuable. The science of the psyche is nowhere near complete, with the current repertoire largely focused on negative states of mind rather than how one might find personal fulfillment. Responsibly working on your self-development is an act of putting your oxygen mask on first with an expectation that you'll help others with their oxygen masks afterwards. 

Contributing towards a better society is still extremely virtuous and is encouraged within an authentic life. Even further, in exploring and sharing your own wellbeing, you might create the same bettered society with individuals augmented by your more well-lived paradigm. I believe existence does not necessitate a sacrifice of everything personal in your life towards a higher cause.

This articulation uncovers so much for me. Perhaps I'm simply naive, discovering just one term that philosophers have been discussing for centuries. But I really feel like this is hitting on a concept that communicates, at its core, all of the direction that I have been slowly settling to through every decision I've made and all my retrospection. This has truly been a major influence in my subconsciously-driven path through religion, school, relationships, mental health.

This concept of authenticity is obvious to recognize in your personal life. Your best clothes, travel experiences, dates may be the ones that have been the most natural and raw. You feel inauthenticity too. The distaste for the facade.

As always, applying this to your life is not as easy as talking about it. It is an endless journey towards an unachievable ideal. For me, authenticity is a self-orientation mechanism, a way in which I might wake and work towards myself. No description of someone else's authenticity will help me arrive to my own.

There are a few core concepts I've extracted that I'm using to orient myself. These are my own articulations of what is helpful for this exact position of my lifetime. The focuses I point out drive me closer to my own deeper, purer life. Your relationship to these concepts may be entirely different – this is okay, this is a deeply individual pursuit.

One focus is self-maintenance, because it is too natural for me to instinctively put off anything that I imagine offering even the slightest friction. The consequence of this is a calcifying buildup of unaddressed tasks, unfinished desires, missed opportunities. Living with this backlog does not make me feel proud of myself. It causes me to live distracted. With the buildup I am endlessly multitasking, praying I don't forget something important. Worse, it results in holding shame that I am not executing as I want to be.

Combating this is easy – start doing everything you hate to do. To me, following through on my menial chores often feels like a fleeting success, but it has real and long-lasting impact on my self-image. Exploring opportunities that have long piqued my interest is not only rewarding in opening new, fascinating worlds, but also builds my self-trust.  After caring for my health I look at my body with affection. Life is regret-free. Clearing this backlog is a phenomenal weight off my shoulders and helps me to live with pride. My identity and my care for my identity are aligned.

Another focus is intentionality. I seek awareness, consciousness of my environment, understanding my relation to it and the options available. The realized flow of this focus is a settled, authentic situational ownership in which I lead my moment alongside anyone with me. This is a direct exit from a lifestyle of Sartre's "bad faith". This is seizing the freedom I am condemned to and possessing it, exploring it, even playing with it.

This concept in particular is not as easy to explore safely. I have intimately experienced the pain of extreme self-consciousness and self-awareness, and what I describe encourages self-awareness even further. But importantly, the damage done by self-consciousness most often originates from judgement and desire. There is a rich flavor of self-awareness with no judgement. Recognize whether you are able to be aware of yourself while still remaining objective and detached. If you can't do this, find true satisfaction with your core identity and day-to-day decisions first.

I would classify both of these interests into the first principle from Charles Taylor

In states of both alignment and awareness, I feel as though I am living at the forefront of my existence, embodying my essence, confronting myself. I see myself in my form. I have the clarity to question where I am, and I can begin to decide how I want to be. I'm unable to reliably discern what authenticity is until I am here.
